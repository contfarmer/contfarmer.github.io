“Daniel Lewis Lee: US executes first federal prisoner in 17 years”

Image copyright AFP

US murderer Daniel Lewis Lee has been put to death, hours after the Supreme Court allowed the first executions of federal inmates in 17 years.

Several executions were initially delayed when a judge ruled on Monday that there were still unresolved legal challenges.

The condemned prisoners had argued that lethal injections constitute “cruel and unusual punishments”.

But the Supreme Court voted 5-4 that “executions may proceed as planned”.

Last year, the Trump administration said it would resume federal executions.

Lee was executed by lethal injection in Terre Haute, Indiana, early on Tuesday.

Some relatives of his victims had opposed the execution and sought to have it delayed, arguing that attending it could expose them to coronavirus.

Earlene Peterson, 81, whose daughter, granddaughter and son-in-law were killed by Lee, said she wanted the 47-year-old to be given life in jail, the same sentence as his accomplice.

Who was Daniel Lewis Lee?

Lee was convicted of torturing and killing a family of three in Arkansas in 1996, dumping their bodies in a lake.

Postponed from December, his execution had been rescheduled for 16:00 (20:00 GMT) on 13 July, but was blocked by a ruling from District Judge Tanya Chutkan.

“The court… finds that the likely harm that plaintiffs would suffer if the court does not grant injunctive relief far outweighs any potential harm to defendants,” the judge said.

Her ruling was consequently overruled by the Supreme Court, which said: “The plaintiffs in this case have not made the showing required to justify last-minute intervention by a Federal Court.”

Federal and state executions - what’s the difference?

Under the US justice system, crimes can be tried either in federal courts - at a national level - or in state courts, at a regional level.

Certain crimes, such as counterfeiting currency or mail theft, are automatically tried at a federal level, as are cases in which the US is a party or those which involve constitutional violations. Others can be tried in federal courts based on the severity of the crimes.

The death penalty was outlawed at state and federal level by a 1972 Supreme Court decision that cancelled all existing death penalty statutes.

A 1976 Supreme Court decision allowed states to reinstate the death penalty and in 1988 the government passed legislation that made it available again at federal level.

According to data collected by the Death Penalty Information Center, 78 people were sentenced to death in federal cases between 1988 and 2018 but only three were executed. There are 62 inmates currently on federal death row.

Future executions

The previous inmate executed by federal death penalty was Louis Jones Jr, a 53-year-old Gulf War veteran who murdered 19-year-old soldier Tracie Joy McBride.

Three more federal executions are scheduled in the near future. All three prisoners are, like Lee, child killers:

Wesley Ira Purkey, who was sentenced in Missouri in 2003 for the rape and murder of a 16-year-old girl whose body he dismembered, burnt and dumped in a septic pond, is due to be executed on Wednesday

Dustin Lee Honken, sentenced in Iowa in 2004 for shooting dead five people including two girls aged six and 10, is due to be executed on Friday

Keith Dwayne Nelson, sentenced in Missouri in 2001 for the rape and murder of a 10-year-old girl behind a church, is set to be executed next month

The execution of Lee - and the four others that were delayed - were all set to use an injection containing the drug pentobarbital.

The inmates have argued the drug violates their constitutional right not to have “cruel and unusual punishments inflicted”, because it has allegedly caused additional medical problems in previous uses.

A lawyer for one of the four federal death-row inmates said in a statement on Monday: “The government has been trying to plough forward with these executions despite many unanswered questions about the legality of its new execution protocol.”

Why the change in rules on executions?

The Trump administration said it would resume federal executions after a long hiatus last year.

In a statement at the time, Attorney General William Barr said: “Under administrations of both parties, the Department of Justice has sought the death penalty against the worst criminals.

“The Justice Department upholds the rule of law - and we owe it to the victims and their families to carry forward the sentence imposed by our justice system.”

Original story: Judge delays executions hours before Daniel Lewis Lee is set to die by lethal injection

It will be up to the Supreme Court of the United States to decide whether the execution of a man held at the U.S. Penitentiary in Terre Haute will go forward.

Daniel Lewis Lee, a member of a white supremacist group, murdered a family of three, including an 8-year-old girl, in January 1996 in Arkansas. After robbing and shooting the victims with a stun gun, Lee covered their heads with plastic bags, sealed the bags with duct tape, weighed down each victim with rocks, and threw the family of three into the Illinois Bayou in Arkansas.

Lee was convicted of murder in aid of racketeering three years later and was sentenced to death.

Lee’s death by lethal injection — which would have been the first federal execution in 17 years — was to take place at 4 p.m. Monday. However, due to a series of judicial interventions, resulting in multiple petitions to the Supreme Court, he remained on death row Friday evening.

Court interventions leave inmates, families in limbo

The district court injunction that halted Lee’s execution has paused all scheduled federal executions, including three others that were expected to take place at the Terre Haute facility within the coming weeks.

On late Friday afternoon, a district court ruled in favor of victims’ family members, who petitioned for an injunction to pause the execution because traveling to attend would put them at higher risk of contracting the novel coronavirus. An appeals court reversed that decision on Sunday.

By Monday morning, just hours before the Lee’s execution was scheduled to take place, a district judge had intervened yet again, this time granting a preliminary injunction in a case brought by Lee and a dozen other plaintiffs. In their request for injunction, the plaintiffs argued that the injection violates their Eighth Amendment rights — essentially, that the drug’s effects constitute cruel and unusual punishment. They also proposed alternatives they said would reduce the risk of pain.

The Bureau of Prisons uses a single drug, pentobarbital, in executions. The inmates’ attorneys argued the drug is likely to cause extreme pain, as it induces flash pulmonary edema, producing a drowning sensation. Eyewitness accounts of these executions also describe these prisoners gasping for air and autopsy reports indicate foam or froth in their airways, which indicates the edema fluid mixed with air as the inmate was still attempting to breathe.

They argue that additional safeguards could be taken to reduce the risk of pain, including peripheral IV lines, administering the lethal injection protocol bedside and implementing a contingency plan to respond to “unexpected outcomes.” They also advocate for a pre-dose of opioids to limit any pain. The Bureau of Prisons has already considered and rejected the use of fentanyl, speculating that drug manufacturers would refuse to supply it for this use.

In their request for injunction, Lee and the other plaintiffs argued that execution by firing squad could be considered an alternative that would “significantly reduce the risk of severe pain,” pointing to studies that have shown a competent shooting causes near-instant death and firing squads have the lowest rate of “botched” executions of any method of execution conducted since 1900.

Execution by firing squad is legal in three states, and the last such execution was conducted in June 2010 in Utah.

In her opinion, U.S. District Judge Tanya Chutkan of the District of Columbia wrote that the injunction is in the public interest, because “the public is not served by short-circuiting legitimate judicial process, and is greatly served by attempting to ensure that the most serious punishment is imposed in a manner consistent with our Constitution.”

While Chutkan did not offer an opinion as to whether firing squads would be an acceptable alternative in every case, she did note that the prisons bureau could readily accommodate the proposal: “the federal government is uniquely capable of consulting with Utah and adopting its existing protocol.”

This is the second time Chutkan has ordered an injunction in this case, according to The Hill. The first, last November, found new execution protocols likely violated federal death penalty laws, and was overturned by an appeals court and was not heard by the U.S. Supreme Court.

In response to Monday’s injunction, the Department of Justice notified the court of its intent to appeal as well as a motion asking for a stay of the injunction until that appeal could be heard, which would allow for three of the four executions scheduled at the Terre Haute facility to move forward.

In its motion to stay, the DOJ argues the plaintiffs did not propose “a known and available alternative method of execution that is feasible and readily implemented.”

Further, the DOJ argues, each of these executions requires the activation of an “execution team” of about 40 Bureau of Prisons staffers and the mobilization of approximately 100 staffers to provide security and support during the execution. Delaying the execution, they argue, “would require significant planning and coordination such as that which already has been undertaken by BOP to date.”

“This is particularly the case now that we are merely hours from Lee’s execution and two more inmates’ executions are only a few days away,” they continued. “Accordingly, beyond the Government’s strong interest in proceeding with the criminal judgment, operational considerations surrounding the planned executions lend further support in favor of a stay.”

Appealing to the Supreme Court

Some of the victims’ family members, Lee and his counsel and the DOJ asked SCOTUS to intervene Monday — all for different reasons.

The DOJ doubled down on its claims that Lee and the other plaintiffs had not provided reasonable alternatives and chastised the district court for the timing of its order: “The district court’s last-minute, day-of-execution injunction is inappropriate, contrary to binding precedent from this Court, and should be promptly stayed or summarily vacated.”

The family has repeatedly argued — and did so in an emergency application to stay sent to the Supreme Court Monday — that traveling to attend the execution would put them at higher risk of contracting the novel coronavirus: “these family members have no interest in asserting their right to attend the execution and seeking to postpone the execution until the resurgence of the COVID-19 virus abates and they can travel safely.”

Shawn Nolan, an attorney representing one of the more than a dozen intervenors or plaintiffs named in the lawsuit, said in a written statement Monday afternoon that the district court’s injunction would allow the courts to address the issues raised regarding the legality of execution protocols.

“Given that these executions threaten to become COVID-19 super-spreader events,” Nolan added, “the injunction will also protect the lives and health of the correctional staff, victim family members, spiritual advisors, attorneys, and others who must witness the executions.”

Further, Earlene Peterson, the 81-year-old mother and grandmother of two of Lee’s victims, has argued that Lee’s co-defendant, Chevie Kehoe, was the ringleader on that fateful day. She said that because the co-defendant was sentenced to life in prison, Lee should receive the same.

“As a supporter of President Trump, I pray that he will hear my message: the scheduled execution of Danny Lee for the murder of my daughter and granddaughter is not what I want and would bring my family more pain,” Peterson said in a written statement late last month. “We don’t want Danny Lee to be executed.”

Lee’s attorneys on Monday submitted a petition asking the Supreme Court to review the legality of his sentence.

“The sentencing disparity between Kehoe and Lee, along with serious questions about the evidence presented to the jury both at trial and sentencing and the government’s course of conduct during Lee’s postconviction proceedings, underscores how denying relief to Lee—who faces execution to carry out a sentence of death—risks severe injustice,” the motion states.

As of publication, the stay stands. The Supreme Court has not yet responded to the petitions.

Protesters voice pro- and anti-execution sentiments

As the case volleyed between courts and judges Monday afternoon, protests — both in support of and against the death penalty — took place in the hours before Lee’s scheduled execution.

The group of anti-death penalty protesters made their presence known at the busy intersection of U.S. 41 and East Springhill Drive in Terre Haute, about 2 miles from the prison.

About 20 people arrived shortly before 3 p.m. and remained in the southwest corner of the intersection. Some held large banners that read “STOP STATE KILLING” and “LOVE MERCY JUSTICE.”

The protesters repeatedly checked their phones for updates on the status of the execution. They were all aware of how rapidly the situation could change and were ready to adjust if needed.

Bill Pelke’s grandmother Ruth Pelke was stabbed to death in northwest Indiana in 1985. Paula Cooper, who was 16 at the time, was sentenced to death for Pelke’s murder. At the time, Cooper was the youngest inmate on death row.

Bill Pelke traveled from Alaska to join the anti-death penalty protest outside of the prison. He said the executions don’t do much for the victims families’ healing process.

“I talk about love and compassion for all humanity. You don’t need to see somebody else die to heal,” Pelke said.

Cooper was released in 2013 and later died by suicide.

Abraham Bonowitz, one of the directors of the Terre Haute Death Penalty Resistance and protest organizer, said executions fail as a public policy.

“It’s a moral failure and we’re here to stand for everyone who opposes the death penalty and not tucked away where no one can see us,” he said.

Reaction to the anti-death penalty group from passing drivers was mixed. Some motorists honked their horns and pumped their fists in support. Others yelled insults.

One woman yelled “Fry ‘em, fry the bastards!” from the passenger seat of a blue sedan. A man driving a red pickup truck waiting at the intersection revved his engine loudly and blanketed the crowd of protesters in thick, black exhaust smoke as he pulled off.

That same pickup driver returned about 15 minutes later to blast the group with exhaust a second time.

Tyler McDuffie, a Brazil resident, demonstrated in favor of the execution. He saw protesters at the corner while on his way to go fishing with a family member.

His decision to demonstrate was a spontaneous one. He pulled into a parking lot across the street and quickly made a sign that read “CONTINUE EXECUTION. AN EYE FOR EYE” with a marker and a part of a FexEx box.
